Judicial Recusal

Judges are supposed to be neutral, fair, and free of bias. But what if they’re not? What if your judge used to work with the opposing attorney, has a grudge, or has personal involvement in your case?

You have the right to request judicial recusal — and you don’t have to be a lawyer to do it.

🧾 When to File for Recusal

  • ⚖️ The judge has a personal or financial interest in the case
  • 👥 The judge has a personal relationship with one of the parties or attorneys
  • 🧠 The judge has demonstrated clear bias, hostility, or prejudice
  • 📑 You have documentation (prior involvement, public statements, etc.)

🛠 How to File a Recusal Motion

  • 📝 Write a motion for judicial recusal or disqualification
  • 📂 Include detailed facts and evidence (dates, quotes, interactions)
  • 📎 Attach any supporting documents, screenshots, or records
  • 📬 File it with the court clerk and serve all parties
  • 🧭 Follow your local or state rule (e.g., Virginia Code § 19.2-271.6)

💡 Pro Tips

  • 🧠 Be respectful but firm — don’t accuse, but assert facts
  • 🔍 Ask Sage for a sample motion or local rule in your state
  • 📣 If denied, you may still preserve the objection for appeal

⚠️ Judges are human. They make mistakes — and sometimes those mistakes are personal. You’re not crazy for noticing. You’re sovereign for standing up.
